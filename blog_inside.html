What does it take to become SOC Analyst?

Text Javokhir Date 10.Oct 2025 Read 1 Min

SOC Analysis, also known as Security Operations Center Analysis, focuses on monitoring and defending an organization’s IT systems. SOC Analysts track network traffic, system logs, and security alerts to spot unusual activity. They use tools like SIEM, firewalls, and endpoint protection to detect threats in real time. Their main tasks include analyzing incidents, responding quickly, and preventing cyberattacks. This role is important because it helps reduce risks, protect sensitive data, and keep businesses running safely.

Monitoring:

Monitoring is the primary duty of a Level 1 SOC Analyst and serves as the frontline defense of the security team. It involves constantly observing SIEM dashboards, firewalls, IDS/IPS, and other monitoring tools to ensure continuous visibility across the organization’s IT environment. Monitoring is not only about detecting alerts but also about understanding the normal behavior of systems and users so that unusual patterns stand out. An effective L1 Analyst must stay alert at all times, since even small signs of suspicious activity can be early warnings of a cyberattack.

Analysis:

Analysis comes after monitoring, when an alert or unusual event requires deeper review. L1 Analysts investigate system logs, network traffic, and user activity to determine if the alert represents a real threat. This process demands critical thinking and pattern recognition, as well as knowledge of common cyberattacks such as phishing, brute-force attempts, or malware infections. By separating false positives from genuine threats, the analyst helps the SOC operate more efficiently and ensures security teams respond only to issues that matter.

Triage:

Triage is the decision-making process that helps SOC teams manage multiple incidents at once. Not all threats carry the same level of risk, so triage involves classifying each incident as low, medium, or high priority. For example, repeated failed login attempts might be labeled low or medium risk, while signs of ransomware activity would be escalated to high risk immediately. Triage ensures that security resources are used wisely, that urgent threats get immediate attention, and that nothing is overlooked during busy shifts.

Escalation:

Escalation is the final step when L1 Analysts reach the limits of their authority or technical expertise. If an incident is too complex, requires advanced forensics, or involves high-risk systems, the analyst collects all relevant data—such as logs, IP addresses, timelines, and initial findings—and passes the case to Level 2 or Level 3 analysts. This process ensures that senior analysts have the context needed to investigate quickly and effectively. Escalation is also about communication and teamwork, since clear handovers reduce mistakes and speed up incident resolution.

Together, these four responsibilities—monitoring, analysis, triage, and escalation—form the core of the L1 SOC Analyst role. While the position is often considered entry-level, it is essential for the daily security posture of an organization. L1 Analysts act as the eyes and ears of the SOC, ensuring that potential threats are detected, studied, prioritized, and reported without delay. By handling routine alerts and escalating major incidents, they give higher-level analysts the time and focus needed to stop serious attacks. In short, L1 SOC Analysts keep the organization safe by being the first responders in the fight against cyber threats.
